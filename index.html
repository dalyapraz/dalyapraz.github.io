Research

LOLSec in Russian Language eCrime Landscape

In this paper, we advocate for a comprehensive approach to threat analysis, especially concerning state-linked eCrime groups. We emphasize the importance of viewing eCrime as a sociotechnical system, necessitating a blend of linguistic, regional, professional, and technical insights. Using the Conti ransomware group's discourse during the Russian invasion of Ukraine as a case study, we highlight how a purely technical perspective may overlook crucial cultural and linguistic nuances like slang and humor. We demonstrate that without proper linguistic and domain knowledge, the discourse and threats from these groups can be misconstrued.

Building and Testing a Network of Social Trust in an Underground Forum

Underground markets/forums facilitate e-crime by allowing buyers and merchants to trade assets using digital currencies, payment providers, and wallets. The anonymity of these markets makes it difficult to study trust in these ecosystems. We offer an empirical analysis of an underground forum to understand online crime, examining interactions in the social network that support traditional, transitional, and new crimes. Our results show that removing highly connected moderators or contributors will not harm the network unless a significant percentage is removed.

Designing a Vulnerability Management Dashboard

Network vulnerability management reduces threats from software, hardware, or organizational weaknesses. To overcome the challenges of analyzing large amounts of data and communicating threats efficiently, we're developing a vulnerability management dashboard for security analysts. Our approach uses real-world data and interviews to design a dashboard that prioritizes vulnerabilities by age, persistence, and impact on the system.
